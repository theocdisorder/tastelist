My TV Taste Profile
Prestige Drama Satire Crime Dark Comedy Writer-Led

A taste that spans the full prestige-drama canon alongside some of the sharpest comedy ever made for television — and takes both equally seriously.

The drama selections (Breaking Bad, The Wire, Succession, Game of Thrones, Friday Night Lights) share a preoccupation with power, moral collapse, and institutions failing their people. The comedy picks (30 Rock, Veep, Arrested Development, Seinfeld, Community) are all writer-driven, densely layered, and reward rewatching.

The common thread across both: intelligence on the page. Whether it's Vince Gilligan's plotting or Armando Iannucci's political satire, the writing always comes first.

Across everything
Sharp writing
The non-negotiable. Austen's surgical social precision, Succession landing every line, Tarantino's dialogue. The writing earns everything else — if it isn't on the page, it isn't on the screen.

Film & TV
Earned tension
Not manufactured suspense — methodical, psychological pressure that builds because you understand exactly what's at stake. Edge of your seat because the writing and performances put you there.
Oldboy Breaking Bad Gone Girl Nightcrawler Fight Club The Wire
Books, film & TV
Strong characters
Morally complex people with real interior lives. Characters who make decisions you feel, not just observe — whose choices have weight because you understand exactly who they are.
The Wire A Separation Pachinko BoJack Horseman Harry Potter Austen heroines
Music
Melodic architecture
Songs built to go somewhere. A hook that earns its place, a chorus that actually arrives, a crescendo that pays off everything before it. The quiet parts exist to make the loud parts hit harder.
Pink Floyd Muse ABBA Radiohead Queen The Strokes

Art that makes you feel something you didn't expect to feel that deeply. Not sentimentality — a genuine emotional arc that lands because everything leading up to it was earned.

Art that has a position and commits to it. Ayn Rand reshaping how you think about merit and individual will. Camus on the absurd. Pan's Labyrinth on what stories are actually for.
